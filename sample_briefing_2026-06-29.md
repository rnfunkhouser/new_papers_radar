## 1. LLMs are about as persuasive as a campaign ad — but they don't scale yet
**Chen, Kalla, Le, Nakamura-Sakai, Sekhon & Wang** · *Journal of Experimental Political Science* · June 4, 2026
[doi.org/10.1017/xps.2026.10032](https://doi.org/10.1017/xps.2026.10032)

The fear that chatbots will supercharge political persuasion gets its first serious cost-accounting here, and the answer is more measured than the panic. Across two survey experiments (**N = 10,417**) plus real-world simulations, the authors separate the two steps persuasion actually requires — getting the message *received* and getting it *accepted* — and price each out. Once a voter actually reads an LLM's pitch, it's about as persuasive as a conventional campaign ad. On a per-voter basis it even looks cheap: **$48–75 versus ~$100** for traditional tactics. The catch is distribution. Traditional methods still scale far more effectively, because there's no cheap way to *get* millions of people to sit and interact with a chatbot. So the conclusion is a "not yet, but watch this": LLMs don't currently offer a step-change in mass persuasion, but that math flips the moment scalable exposure becomes feasible.

Why it's worth your time: Kalla (of the door-to-door canvassing literature) co-authoring means the persuasion measurement is taken seriously, and the receive/accept decomposition is a cleaner causal frame than most of the "AI will destroy democracy" discourse. This is the paper to cite when you want to *deflate* hype without dismissing the threat.

## 2. Inoculation against vaccine disinformation works — but only if you segment the audience first
**Chou & Boman** · *Vaccine* · June 2026
[doi.org/10.1016/j.vaccine.2026.128749](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.vaccine.2026.128749)

A nice corrective to one-size-fits-all prebunking. Using a motivation–knowledge–attitude framework, the authors sort American adults into four publics — **disinformation-immune, vulnerable, receptive, and amplifying** — then expose them to one of three inoculation messages: autonomy-*supportive*, autonomy-*controlling*, or a control. Both inoculation styles raised "motivational threat" well above control (p < .001), and the vulnerable and amplifying publics felt that threat most. But the interesting twist is the **significant segment × message interaction** (p = .005): the effect on perceived message effectiveness was driven mainly by the *already-immune* public, while message *style* barely differentiated among the other segments. In other words, the people most in need of inoculation are the least moved by how you frame it — a finding that complicates the autonomy-supportive-messaging orthodoxy and argues for audience segmentation as a design step, not an afterthought.

Why it's worth your time: directly relevant to your inoculation/prebunking cluster (van der Linden, Roozenbeek, Simchon in your seed set), and it pushes on the *boundary conditions* rather than re-confirming that inoculation "works."

## 5. Preprint — Inoculation sharpens *discernment* but barely moves *identity-driven* attitudes
**(PsyArXiv preprint)** · May 8, 2026
[doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/t4ewc_v2](https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/t4ewc_v2)
